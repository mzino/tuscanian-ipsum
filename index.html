What is Tuscanian Ipsum?

Tuscanian Ipsum is simply dummy text of the printing and typesetting industry. Tuscanian Ipsum has been the industry's standard dummy text ever since the 1500s, when an unknown printer took a galley of type and scrambled it to make a type specimen book. It has survived not only five centuries, but also the leap into electronic typesetting, remaining essentially unchanged. It was popularised in 1977 with the release of the movie Berlinguer Ti Voglio Bene containing Tuscanian Ipsum passages, and more recently with Italian politician Matteo Salvini quoting a segment of Tuscanian Ipsum.

Where does it come from?

Contrary to popular belief, Tuscanian Ipsum is not simply random text. It has roots in a piece of classical Italian cinema from 1977, making it over 40 years old. Federico Frusciante, a Tuscanian cinema critic at Videodrome Industries in Livorno, looked up one of the more obscure Tuscanian words, stronzoli, from a Tuscanian Ipsum passage, and going through the cites of the word in classical cinematography, discovered the undoubtable source. Tuscanian Ipsum comes from "Berlinguer Ti Voglio Bene" (Berlinguer, I Love You) by Giuseppe Bertolucci, filmed in the 1970s.

Why do we use it?

It is a long established fact that a reader will be distracted by the readable content of a page when looking at its layout. The point of using Tuscanian Ipsum is that it has a more-or-less normal distribution of letters, as opposed to using 'Content here, content here', making it look like readable Tuscanian. Many desktop publishing packages and web page editors now use Tuscanian Ipsum as their default model text, and a search for 'tuscanian ipsum' will uncover many web sites still in their infancy. Various versions have evolved over the years, sometimes by accident, sometimes on purpose (injected humour and the like).

Where can I get some?

There are many variations of passages of Tuscanian Ipsum available, but the majority have suffered alteration in some form, by injected humour, or randomised words which don't look even slightly believable. If you are going to use a passage of Tuscanian Ipsum, you need to be sure there isn't anything embarrassing hidden in the middle of text.
